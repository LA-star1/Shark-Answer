CIE A-Level Chemistry (9701) — Examiner-Approved Terminology Rules
=======================================================================
These rules encode EXACT wording required by CIE mark schemes.
Deviating from these terms will lose marks even if the underlying
understanding is correct. Apply them precisely in every answer.

SECTION 1: HALOGEN COLOURS AT 293 K (room temperature)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
CRITICAL: The question asks for colour of the ELEMENT at 293 K (solid/liquid/gas
as it naturally exists at room temperature and pressure).

  Halogen     Physical state at 293 K    CIE-required colour
  ─────────   ───────────────────────    ───────────────────
  Fluorine    gas                        pale yellow (accept: yellow)
  Chlorine    gas                        green (accept: yellow-green, greenish-yellow)
  Bromine     liquid                     brown (accept: red-brown, dark red)
  Iodine      SOLID                      (dark) grey  ← CRITICAL: NOT violet/purple
  Astatine    solid                      (dark) grey / black

⚠️  COMMON MODEL ERROR — IODINE:
    "violet" / "purple" = colour of IODINE VAPOUR or iodine in non-polar solvents
    At 293 K, iodine exists as a SOLID → colour = (dark) GREY (with metallic lustre)
    Writing "violet" for iodine at 293 K = WRONG → 0 marks for the whole table row

⚠️  ALL THREE must be correct for the mark:
    If chlorine, bromine AND iodine colours are all required in one question,
    CIE awards the mark only when ALL THREE are correct simultaneously.

Halogen colour in solution (separate from physical state questions):
  Chlorine water:           pale yellow / yellow-green
  Bromine water:            orange / yellow-orange
  Iodine in water:          brown (trace amounts give yellow)
  Iodine in cyclohexane:    violet / purple  (non-polar solvent → violet)
  Iodine in KI(aq):         brown (triiodide I₃⁻ complex)

SECTION 2: HALOGEN REACTIVITY AND DISPLACEMENT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Oxidising power order (strongest first):  F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂

Displacement reactions (more reactive halogen displaces less reactive halide):
  Cl₂ + 2Br⁻ → 2Cl⁻ + Br₂    (chlorine displaces bromide)
  Cl₂ + 2I⁻  → 2Cl⁻ + I₂     (chlorine displaces iodide)
  Br₂ + 2I⁻  → 2Br⁻ + I₂     (bromine displaces iodide)
  Br₂ does NOT displace Cl⁻ (bromine is weaker oxidising agent than chlorine)
  I₂ does NOT displace Cl⁻ or Br⁻

Halide reducing power order (strongest reducer first): I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻

Reactions of halides with concentrated H₂SO₄:
  NaF/NaCl + conc H₂SO₄:  produces HF/HCl only (no redox; F⁻, Cl⁻ too weak reducers)
  NaBr + conc H₂SO₄:       HBr + SO₂ + Br₂ + H₂O  (Br⁻ reduces S(VI) to S(IV))
  NaI + conc H₂SO₄:        HI + H₂S + I₂ + SO₂ + S  (I⁻ reduces S all the way to H₂S)

CIE required observation for NaI + conc H₂SO₄:
  "Purple/violet fumes" (iodine vapour), "yellow solid" (sulphur), "rotten egg smell" (H₂S)

SECTION 3: ACID-BASE AND IONIC EQUILIBRIA TERMINOLOGY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Buffer solution definition (CIE-approved):
  "A solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or alkali
   are added."  [NOT "maintains constant pH" — that is penalised]

Strong acid definition: "An acid that is FULLY/COMPLETELY dissociated in aqueous
  solution." (NOT "ionised" alone without "completely")

Weak acid definition: "An acid that is PARTIALLY dissociated in aqueous solution."

Le Chatelier's Principle (must include all three elements to gain full credit):
  "When a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change [in concentration /
   temperature / pressure], the equilibrium shifts in the direction that
   opposes / minimises the effect of that change."

SECTION 4: ELECTRONEGATIVITY AND BONDING
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Electronegativity trend: increases across a period, decreases down a group.
Most electronegative element: Fluorine (F).

Hydrogen bonding requirements (CIE mark scheme checklist):
  ✓ Bond between H and a highly electronegative atom (N, O, or F)
  ✓ Lone pair on N, O, or F of the ACCEPTOR molecule
  ✓ Drawn as dashed line H···X (X = N, O, F) — NOT a solid line
  ✓ Correct directionality shown (lone pair → H)

van der Waals / London dispersion forces:
  Correct term: "instantaneous dipole – induced dipole forces"  (or London forces)
  NOT acceptable alone: "van der Waals" without further qualification when
  the question specifically asks about the type of intermolecular force.

SECTION 5: ORGANIC CHEMISTRY MECHANISMS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Mechanism labels must match CIE terminology exactly:
  Electrophilic addition (EA):       alkenes + Br₂, HBr, H₂O(H⁺)
  Electrophilic substitution (ES):   benzene + Br₂(AlBr₃), HNO₃(H₂SO₄)
  Nucleophilic substitution (NS):    halogenoalkanes + NaOH, KCN, NH₃
  Nucleophilic addition (NA):        aldehydes/ketones + HCN, NaBH₄
  Free-radical substitution (FRS):   alkanes + Cl₂/Br₂ (UV light)
  Elimination:                       halogenoalkanes + KOH/ethanol (hot, conc)

Curly arrow rules (penalised if wrong):
  ✓ Arrow FROM lone pair or bond TO electrophile / new bond
  ✓ Arrow head points to where electrons GO
  ✗ Arrows must NOT come from atoms — they come from electron pairs/bonds

SECTION 6: KINETICS AND ENERGETICS PRECISION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Activation energy definition:
  "The minimum energy required for a reaction to occur." OR
  "The minimum energy that colliding particles must have for a successful collision."

Catalyst mechanism (must state both effects):
  "A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway of lower activation energy."
  [Must mention BOTH 'alternative pathway' AND 'lower activation energy']

Rate-determining step: the SLOWEST step in a reaction mechanism.
  Order of reaction = sum of powers of concentrations in the rate-determining step.

Enthalpy change of formation: "The enthalpy change when ONE MOLE of a compound
  is formed from its elements in their standard states under standard conditions
  (298 K, 100 kPa)."

Hess's Law: "The enthalpy change of a reaction is independent of the route taken,
  provided the initial and final states are the same."

SECTION 7: FREQUENTLY PENALISED MISTAKES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
The following phrasings are PENALISED by CIE examiners:

  ✗ "The reaction speeds up" → say "the rate of reaction increases"
  ✗ "More concentrated" for gases → say "higher pressure" or "greater concentration"
  ✗ "Iodine is violet" (solid state) → say "iodine is (dark) grey"
  ✗ "Strong acid has low pH" → state "completely dissociated"
  ✗ "The equilibrium moves right" alone → add "to increase [product/reactant]"
  ✗ "Atoms" when you mean "ions" in ionic equations
  ✗ Omitting state symbols when the question says "give state symbols"
  ✗ "Shared pair of electrons" for dative bonds → say "coordinate bond" or
    "dative covalent bond" (both electrons donated by one atom)
  ✗ Drawing structural formulae without showing all bonds when "displayed formula"
    is requested
